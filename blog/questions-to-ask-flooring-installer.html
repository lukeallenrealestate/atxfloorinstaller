10 Questions to Ask a Flooring Installer

Hiring a flooring installer is one of the bigger decisions you'll make during a home renovation. The material you choose matters, but the quality of the installation matters just as much, if not more. A beautiful hardwood floor installed poorly will gap, buckle, and creak. A budget-friendly LVP installed by a skilled crew will look great and last for years.

The Austin market has hundreds of flooring contractors, ranging from experienced professionals with decades of work to unlicensed handymen advertising on social media. Asking the right questions before you hire helps you separate the two. Here are the ten questions we recommend, along with what a good answer sounds like.

1. Are You Licensed and Insured?

This is the most important question and should always come first. In Texas, flooring installers aren't required to hold a state-specific flooring license, but they should carry general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage. Liability insurance protects your home if something is damaged during installation. Workers' comp protects you from liability if a worker is injured on your property.

Ask to see a current certificate of insurance, not just a verbal confirmation. A reputable installer will have no problem providing this. If someone hesitates or says they "don't need insurance," that's your first red flag.

2. How Long Have You Been Installing Flooring?

Experience matters in flooring more than most trades. An installer who's been working for 10 or 15 years has seen every type of subfloor condition, dealt with humidity issues specific to Texas, and knows how different materials behave in real-world conditions. A newer installer may do fine with straightforward LVP click-lock in a square room, but complex projects like hardwood on stairs, herringbone patterns, or large open-concept layouts require years of practice to execute well.

There's no magic number, but be cautious with anyone who has fewer than three years of dedicated flooring experience. Ask about specific project types they've completed, not just how many years they've been in business.

3. Can I See Recent Local References?

Online reviews are helpful but can be manipulated. Asking for three to five recent references from projects in the Austin area gives you real homeowners to talk to. When you call those references, ask specific questions: Did the crew show up on time? Was the final price close to the estimate? How does the floor look six months or a year later? Would you hire them again?

A contractor who can't provide local references, or who offers references that are all more than two years old, may not have a consistent track record. Recent work in your area is the most reliable indicator of what your experience will be.

4. What's Included in Your Quote?

This question prevents the most common source of disputes between homeowners and installers: unexpected costs. A complete flooring quote should itemize:

  • Material costs (flooring, underlayment, adhesive, transitions)
  • Labor costs
  • Furniture moving (if applicable)
  • Old flooring removal and disposal
  • Subfloor preparation or repair
  • Baseboards and quarter-round removal and reinstallation
  • Threshold transitions between rooms

If a quote is just a single number with no breakdown, ask for details. The most common hidden costs in flooring projects are subfloor repair, old flooring removal, and trim work. These can easily add $1,000 to $3,000 to a project if they weren't included in the original estimate.

5. Who Does the Actual Installation — Your Crew or Subcontractors?

Some flooring companies use their own trained employees for every job. Others act as brokers, selling the project and then subcontracting the labor to whoever is available that week. Both models can work, but you deserve to know which one you're getting.

When a company uses its own crew, there's more accountability and consistency. The installers are trained to that company's standards, and the company has direct control over quality. When subcontractors are used, the quality can vary from job to job depending on which crew shows up. If the company does use subs, ask if they use the same crews regularly and whether those subs are insured.

6. How Do You Handle Subfloor Issues?

The subfloor is the foundation of every flooring installation, and problems here cause problems everywhere. In Austin, common subfloor issues include uneven concrete slabs, moisture in concrete, squeaky plywood subfloors in older homes, and damaged OSB in homes that have experienced water leaks.

A good installer will tell you they assess the subfloor before starting and will discuss any necessary repairs before proceeding. They should mention moisture testing for concrete slabs and leveling for uneven surfaces. If an installer says they just "install over whatever is there," they're cutting corners that will show up in the finished product.

7. What Warranty Do You Offer on Your Work?

Flooring materials come with manufacturer warranties, but those only cover defects in the product itself, not installation errors. Your installer should offer a separate labor warranty that covers their workmanship. Industry standard for quality installers is one to two years on labor, though some offer longer.

Ask what the warranty covers specifically. Does it include gaps that develop after installation? Planks that come loose? Transitions that fail? Get the warranty terms in writing before work begins. A verbal "we'll take care of any problems" means nothing if the company disputes what qualifies as a problem.

8. How Do You Protect My Home During Installation?

Flooring installation generates dust, debris, and heavy foot traffic. Professional installers take steps to protect the areas of your home that aren't being worked on. This includes covering doorways with plastic sheeting to contain dust, laying protective covering on finished floors that the crew walks across, and using drop cloths in hallways and staircases.

Ask specifically about dust management during demo and cutting. Some installers cut materials outside to keep dust out of your home. Others use dust-collection systems attached to their saws. If an installer doesn't mention any protective measures, expect to spend a day cleaning after they leave.

9. What Brands Do You Recommend and Why?

This question reveals whether an installer genuinely knows their products or just sells whatever has the highest margin. A knowledgeable installer will recommend specific brands based on your needs and explain why. They should be able to discuss wear layers, core construction, warranty differences, and how specific products perform in Austin's climate.

Be cautious of installers who only push one brand, especially if it's one you haven't heard of. Some contractors use off-brand or closeout materials that lack proper warranties. On the other hand, an installer who's brand-agnostic and helps you compare options based on performance and budget is working in your interest.

10. What's Your Timeline for This Project?

A reliable installer should give you a clear timeline that includes when materials will be delivered, how many days the acclimation period will take (for hardwood), when the crew will start, and when they expect to finish. For a typical 1,000-square-foot LVP installation, the work itself takes one to two days. Hardwood installations take longer, and refinishing projects add drying time.

Ask about their current backlog. In Austin's busy spring and summer seasons, wait times of two to four weeks are normal for reputable installers. If someone can start tomorrow, it might mean they don't have steady work, which could indicate quality or reputation issues. Conversely, if someone is booked out three months or more, they're likely in high demand for good reason.

Red Flags to Watch For

Beyond the ten questions above, watch for these warning signs when evaluating flooring contractors in Austin:

  • Demands full payment upfront: A reasonable deposit is normal, typically 30% to 50% for material ordering. But asking for 100% before work begins is a major red flag.
  • No written contract: Every detail should be documented. Verbal agreements lead to disputes. If an installer resists putting terms in writing, walk away.
  • Price is dramatically lower than competitors: If one bid comes in 40% below everyone else, something is being cut. It's usually material quality, proper subfloor prep, or the installer plans to rush the job.
  • Can't show you completed work: Any experienced installer should have photos of recent projects or be willing to let you see a current job site. No portfolio suggests limited experience.
  • Pressures you to decide immediately: "This price is only good today" is a high-pressure sales tactic, not how professional contractors operate. A fair quote should be valid for at least 30 days.
  • No physical business presence: A contractor who only communicates through a personal cell phone and has no business address, website, or Google listing may not be established enough to honor a warranty.

Why the Cheapest Bid Often Costs More

We understand that budget matters. Flooring is a significant investment, and it's natural to compare prices. But the cheapest bid in flooring almost always costs more in the long run. Low-cost installers cut corners on subfloor preparation, skip acclimation, use inferior adhesives, and rush through installations. The results show up within months: gaps, squeaks, loose planks, and failed transitions.

Fixing a bad installation typically costs 50% to 100% of what a proper installation would have cost in the first place, because the material often needs to be replaced entirely. We regularly redo floors in Austin that were installed by the cheapest bidder less than a year ago.

The best value is a competitive price from an experienced, insured installer who communicates clearly, provides a detailed written quote, and stands behind their work with a real warranty.
